## 1. BUSINESS OVERVIEW

Nu Holdings (Nubank) is the world's largest independent digital bank by customer count, operating primarily in Brazil, Mexico, and Colombia. Founded in 2013 by David Vélez (Colombian entrepreneur, ex-Sequoia), the company IPO'd on NYSE in December 2021 at a $41.5B valuation.

**What they do:** No-fee credit cards, personal loans, digital accounts, insurance, investments, and business banking — all via a mobile app. Zero physical branches. The moat is a radically lower cost structure: Nu's cost to serve per active customer is ~$0.80/month vs. an estimated 85% higher cost at Brazilian incumbents (Itaú, Bradesco, Santander Brasil). Customer acquisition cost (CAC) was $6.42 in 2024 — among the lowest in consumer fintech globally.

**Geographic mix (FY2025):** Brazil ~85% of revenue ($11.0B), Mexico ~5% ($808M+), Colombia and other ~5% ($237M+). The investment case is overwhelmingly a Brazil bet.

**Key product levers:**
- Credit card: largest product, generates interchange + interest income
- Nu Credit (personal loans): higher-yield, expanding share
- NuInvest: growing AUM business
- Mexico expansion: 15M+ customers, accelerating
- AI rebuild: "NuFormer" proprietary foundation models — already in production for credit decisioning (Q1 2026 call)

## 4. EARNINGS QUALITY ASSESSMENT

**FCF/Net Income ratio: 1.21x (FY2024)** — GOOD. FCF exceeds net income, consistent across years. This reduces concern about accrual manipulation.

**Q1 2026 RED FLAG — Provisions surge:** EPS of $0.18 missed consensus $0.20 by ~10%. The primary driver was a 33% QoQ jump in credit loss provisions. Management characterized this as (1) normal Q1 seasonality, (2) portfolio growth, and (3) deliberate expansion into higher-risk borrower segments. At face value, Q1 is typically the weakest credit quarter in Brazil (post-holiday spending). However, the magnitude of the provision increase exceeded analyst expectations, suggesting either model conservatism or emerging credit deterioration. This warrants close scrutiny.

**GAAP vs. Non-GAAP:** Nu reports "Managerial Net Income" ($871.4M in Q1 2026) alongside GAAP net income. The key adjustment involves share-based compensation and certain non-cash items. The spread between GAAP and managerial figures is modest and typical of high-growth tech/fintech companies. No evidence of aggressive one-time gains inflating reported earnings.

**Efficiency ratio of 20%** is genuinely exceptional — best-in-class globally among major banks. This is not a one-quarter achievement but a sustained trend, suggesting structural cost advantage.

## 6. ANNUAL REPORT KEY SECTIONS (20-F 2024/2025)

### Top Growth Drivers
1. **Brazil penetration depth:** Despite 131M customers, Nu still has room to grow ARPAC from $16 toward the $27+ seen in mature cohorts — a multi-year revenue compounding engine
2. **Mexico inflection:** 15M+ customers and accelerating. Mexico is a 130M-person country with even worse banking penetration than Brazil historically
3. **SMB banking:** Early-stage but a natural extension — same low-cost digital model applied to small businesses
4. **NuInvest (investing platform):** AUM growing rapidly; diversifies away from credit risk
5. **AI-driven underwriting:** NuFormer enables better credit decisions at scale, potentially improving risk-adjusted margins

### Competitive Moat
- Customer acquisition cost: $6.42 (vs. traditional banks: $50–100+)
- Monthly cost-to-serve: $0.80 (85% below Brazilian incumbents)
- NPS scores consistently best-in-class in Brazil
- Network effect: 131M customers creates data flywheel for credit decisioning
- Zero-fee model creates loyalty; churn rates extremely low

### Key Risk Factors (from 20-F)
1. **Regulatory risk:** Brazil's central bank (BCB) increasingly active in regulating fintechs. Potential capital requirement increases, interchange fee compression, or credit limits. BCB implemented a credit card installment payment regulation in 2024.
2. **Macroeconomic risk in Brazil:** High interest rate environment (Selic ~13.75% in 2025) increases funding costs and credit stress. Economic downturns hit Brazil hard and disproportionately.
3. **Currency risk:** Substantially all revenues in BRL, MXN, COP — all volatile EM currencies. USD reporting creates a structural translation headwind when EM currencies weaken.
4. **Credit risk:** As Nu expands into thinner credit files and higher-risk segments, NPL trajectories bear watching. Credit-cycle risk is real.
5. **Competition:** Incumbents (Itaú, Bradesco, XP) are investing heavily in digital channels. Also competition from fintechs like PicPay, Mercado Pago, C6 Bank.

## 10. KEY RISKS (Top 5)

### 🔴 Risk 1: FX / EM Currency (HIGH — Ongoing)
Nu earns ~85% of revenue in Brazilian reals (BRL), with additional exposure to Mexican peso (MXN) and Colombian peso (COP). The company reports in USD. In Q4 2024, the BRL depreciated materially vs. USD, causing Nu to miss revenue estimates despite strong local-currency growth. Currency-neutral FY2024 revenue growth was meaningfully higher than reported growth. The BRL has been under pressure due to Brazil's fiscal deficit concerns. A sustained 20% BRL depreciation would reduce reported USD revenue by ~17% with no offsetting factor (costs also BRL-denominated, so margins are partially protected, but USD-reported profits would decline). **This is the single biggest systematic risk for US investors in NU.**

### 🟠 Risk 2: Credit Quality Deterioration (MEDIUM-HIGH — Monitoring Required)
Q1 2026 provisions jumped 33% QoQ. While management attributes this to seasonality, the scale of the increase was above normal seasonal patterns. Nu is deliberately expanding into higher-risk borrower segments (lower-income Brazilians with thin credit files) and growing its personal loan book. If Brazil's unemployment rises or wage growth decelerates, NPL ratios could move materially higher. Nu's high interest rates on credit products provide buffer, but a credit cycle turn would hit revenues and require higher provisions simultaneously.

### 🟠 Risk 3: Brazil Macro / Political Risk (MEDIUM-HIGH)
Brazil's macroeconomic environment remains volatile. The Selic rate at ~13.75% creates funding cost pressure. Brazil's fiscal situation has deteriorated, with debt-to-GDP trending toward 100%. Political risk (government interference in financial sector, particularly given president's left-leaning tendencies) could include price caps on credit card rates, mandatory rate ceilings, or altered bankruptcy laws. The 2024 credit card installment regulation was a direct regulatory intervention.

### 🟡 Risk 4: Regulatory Risk — Banking Supervision (MEDIUM)
As Nu grows to systemic scale in Brazil, regulatory intensity will increase. Brazil's BCB could impose higher capital requirements, restrict certain products, or mandate lower interchange fees (a major revenue source from credit cards). Nu also needs regulatory approvals for its US bank charter application — a capital-intensive, complex undertaking.

### 🟡 Risk 5: Founder Concentration & Governance (MEDIUM)
David Vélez holds ~20% of shares and through multi-class share structure maintains disproportionate voting control. While founder alignment is generally positive, this creates governance risk: the board cannot effectively challenge management strategy, and the company can pursue capital allocation decisions (like the US bank charter) that may not be optimal for shareholders. The August 2025 sale of 33M shares at ~$14 by Vélez himself raises questions about his own assessment of fair value.

**VERDICT: ⚠️ WATCH (Lean BUY)**

Nu Holdings is a genuinely exceptional business — the best-run large-scale digital bank outside of China. At 22x TTM P/E and 16x forward P/E for 40%+ earnings growth, the valuation is reasonable to attractive. The analyst community is overwhelmingly bullish with $18–20 price targets implying 40–55% upside.

The **single reason this is WATCH rather than BUY** is the Q1 2026 EPS miss driven by a 33% QoQ surge in credit provisions. This is the one metric that, if it continues in Q2 2026, could signal the beginning of a credit cycle turn that would simultaneously compress earnings and de-rate the multiple. Brazil's credit culture is inherently riskier than developed-market equivalents, and Nu's deliberate push into higher-risk borrower segments adds vulnerability.

**Recommended action:** Establish a half-position now at ~$13 (within 14% of bear case downside, vs. 29–50% base/bull upside). Watch Q2 2026 results (August 2026) for confirmation that provisions normalize. If NPLs improve and provisions moderate, add to full position. If provisions remain elevated or NPLs worsen, hold or reduce.

**Single biggest risk: BRL depreciation combined with credit quality deterioration** — both happen simultaneously in Brazil recessions and would compound each other significantly in USD-reported results.
